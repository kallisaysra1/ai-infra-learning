# Lecture 01: Mentorship and Leadership Without Authority — Overview

## Leadership without the management ladder

Principal engineers are leaders, but they're not managers. They
don't have direct reports, can't approve PTO, don't write
performance reviews. They lead through influence, not
authority.

This makes the job harder in some ways and easier in others.
Easier: you don't carry the people-management overhead.
Harder: every time you want something to happen, you have to
convince people, not direct them.

This lecture is the orientation: what mentorship and influence
at the principal-engineer level actually require.

## Mentorship as a deliberate practice

At this level, mentorship isn't "I sometimes answer questions."
It's the deliberate transfer of judgment to a small set of
engineers whose careers will benefit from your perspective.

The signs of doing it well:

- You can name your mentees and what each of them is working
  on developing.
- You have regular 1-1s with each of them — every 2-3 weeks
  minimum.
- You give specific feedback in those 1-1s, not just
  encouragement.
- You're willing to push back when their thinking is wrong.
- You celebrate their wins explicitly.
- You're sometimes pulled into their hard moments (career
  decisions, project conflicts).

The signs of doing it badly:

- You "mentor" by Slack response.
- You can't name the development arc your mentees are on.
- You only meet when they ask.
- Your feedback is all positive (signaling either flattery or
  withholding).

## Choosing mentees

Most principal engineers can sustain 3-5 active mentees with
real depth. Past that, the relationships get shallow.

Choose deliberately. Patterns that work:

- One or two senior engineers nearing staff/principal — your
  closest peer-tier development.
- One or two strong mid-level engineers who are stretching
  toward senior — bigger development gap, higher leverage.
- Occasionally, an engineer in a different specialty than
  yours — the relationship forces both of you to articulate
  what's transferable in your judgment.

Be honest about who you can sustain. Saying yes to every
mentorship request produces shallow mentorship for everyone,
including the ones who would have benefited most.

## Influence without authority

Influencing engineers and managers who don't report to you is
the core skill that distinguishes effective principal engineers
from the ones who become titled-but-ignored.

The patterns that work:

**Be the person people want to consult.** When your name comes
up as "let's get their input on this," you have influence. When
your name doesn't come up, you don't. Build the consulting
relationships before you need them.

**Make your reasoning legible.** People follow you when they
understand why you think what you think. The principal engineer
who pronounces conclusions without showing the work earns
distrust over time.

**Disagree well.** You will be in rooms where you disagree
with the consensus. Disagree clearly, with specific reasons,
without disrespecting the people on the other side. Then, when
the decision goes against you, accept the outcome gracefully.

**Pick battles.** You have a finite political budget. Spend it
on the few decisions that genuinely matter rather than every
choice that catches your eye.

**Sponsor more than you advocate.** Sponsorship — actively
creating opportunities for others — produces more durable
influence than advocacy.

## The hardest part: changing your output measure

ICs are measured on what they ship. Principal engineers must
internalize that their output is what *other engineers* ship
over the next 3-5 years, shaped by their judgment.

This is uncomfortable for former heads-down ICs. The pull to
go back to writing code is strong. The signals that you're
contributing are noisier than they used to be.

Patterns that help:

- Keep some IC work to stay technically credible, but recognize
  it's no longer your primary contribution.
- Develop pattern recognition for the moments when your input
  matters most.
- Cultivate relationships with peer principals; they're the
  people who understand the work.

## What this lecture doesn't cover

This is the orientation. Specific topics — running 1-1s with
mentees who report to other managers, structuring sponsorship
relationships, managing your reputation, building a peer
network — each deserve their own treatment.

For working examples and rubrics, see the principal-engineer-
solutions repo, mod-502.

## Further reading

- "Staff Engineer" — Will Larson. The most thoughtful book
  on the principal/staff role.
- "The Mentor's Guide" — Lois Zachary. The discipline of
  mentorship.
- "Multipliers" — Liz Wiseman. Leadership that grows others
  vs. leadership that diminishes them.
- "An Elegant Puzzle" — Larson. Engineering management view
  that pairs with this content.
