# Lecture 01: Hiring and Onboarding — Overview

## Why this deserves a module

Hiring and onboarding compound over years. A team lead who
hires well builds momentum that lasts. One who hires poorly
carries that cost forever — through performance issues,
attrition, and the cultural damage of bad fits.

Most team leads don't take hiring seriously enough early in
their tenure. They delegate the loop to senior engineers,
focus on closing the role rather than picking the right
candidate, and onboard new hires by ad-hoc "ask anyone."

This lecture is the orientation: why hiring deserves your
calendar time and what good looks like.

## The hiring time investment

In a growth phase, hiring will consume 20-30% of a team lead's
time. This is not optional. Hiring badly costs significantly
more than this — every bad hire is six months of misplaced
investment plus the cost of separation and rehiring.

The time is spent on:

- Sourcing: identifying candidates the recruiter wouldn't find.
- Loop design: deciding what each interview covers and who
  runs it.
- Loop execution: running interviews well.
- Debrief facilitation: turning interview signals into a
  hire/no-hire decision.
- Closing: persuading the candidate to accept.

Skipping any one of these dilutes the result.

## The interview loop

A well-designed loop covers five signals:

**Coding ability.** Can they write working code at acceptable
quality, given a non-trivial problem? Done well, this takes
60-90 minutes with a real-feeling problem, not algorithm
trivia.

**System design.** Can they reason about a system at the level
this role requires? Senior engineers should design systems;
junior engineers should be able to discuss tradeoffs at a
component level.

**Collaboration.** Can they work in the team's communication
style? Do they listen, push back appropriately, take feedback?

**Domain skill.** Specific to the role. ML systems work
requires evidence of relevant experience; infrastructure work
requires evidence of operational sensibility.

**Communication.** Can they explain their thinking? Do they
ask clarifying questions? Do they admit when they don't know?

Each signal needs at least two interviewers to control for
individual variance.

## The debrief

The debrief is where the loop's signal becomes a decision.
The pattern that works:

1. Each interviewer states their conclusion *before*
   discussion. (Prevents one strong voice from dominating.)
2. Discussion is on specific evidence, not feelings.
3. The hiring manager owns the decision, but is bound by the
   loop's signal.
4. The default is no-hire. "Maybe" is a no.

Common failures: the senior engineer who carries the loop's
opinion via personal authority, the manager who hires despite
weak signal because they're under headcount pressure, the
team that compromises on bar to fill the seat.

## The bar

Holding the bar is the single hardest part of hiring. Every
team lead faces moments where they want to lower the bar
because they need the headcount. Almost every team lead who
lowers it regrets it.

The patterns that protect the bar:

- The bar is articulated in writing. Vague bars drift.
- Senior interviewers calibrate periodically by reading each
  other's debrief notes.
- The hiring manager doesn't single-handedly decide
  "controversial" hires. Skip-level review for borderline
  cases.
- An empty seat is acceptable; a bad hire isn't.

## Onboarding as a 90-day commitment

Most team leads underestimate onboarding. They think the new
hire will be productive in a week. The reality is 60-90 days
to full productivity, and the team lead's investment during
those days determines whether the new hire reaches it.

The pattern:

Week 1: environment set up; first PR merged (even trivial).
Week 2-3: paired work on a small project. Buddy assigned.
Week 4: first 30-day retro — how's it going?
Week 5-8: owning a small piece of work end-to-end.
Week 9-12: integrated into the team's rotation; primary
owner of one workstream.

Onboarding plans that aren't followed are useless. The team
lead's job is checking in regularly, not just at the 30-day
mark.

## What this lecture doesn't cover

This is the orientation. Specific topics — how to write a
job description, how to run a good debrief, how to handle a
compensation negotiation, how to read engagement signals in
new hires — each deserve their own treatment.
