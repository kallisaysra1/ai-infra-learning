# Lecture 01: People Management Basics — Overview

## The transition that breaks engineers

Most engineers who become managers fail in predictable ways
during their first 18 months. The reasons are structural, not
personal:

- Engineers are trained to optimize *systems*. People aren't
  systems.
- Engineers are rewarded for *output*. Managers are rewarded
  for *output via others*, which is a longer feedback loop with
  noisier signals.
- Engineers are taught *precise communication*. People
  management requires tolerance for ambiguity.
- Engineers default to *fixing the problem themselves*.
  Managers who do this fail to grow their reports.

This lecture is the orientation: what's actually different
about people management, and what the core skills are.

## The job is different

A new manager often thinks of management as "the technical
work plus 1-1s." It isn't. The job has different inputs,
different outputs, and different success criteria.

Inputs change. Where the IC's inputs were a technical problem
and a codebase, the manager's inputs are people's career
aspirations, project pressures, organizational politics, and
emerging conflict. These inputs arrive in unstructured form
and require active attention to notice.

Outputs change. The IC ships code. The manager ships teams
that ship code. The output is one level of indirection away,
which means the feedback loop is one level slower.

Success criteria change. The IC is measured on what they
shipped. The manager is measured on what their team shipped,
how their team is growing, and how their team is perceived by
the rest of the organization. These three goals can conflict.

## The core skills

People management at the first-line level rests on five core
skills.

**Listening well.** Most management problems start as something
a report tried to say that the manager half-heard. Learning to
listen — to the words and the subtext — is the foundational
skill.

**Asking good questions.** A manager who tells reports what to
do scales linearly. A manager who asks the right questions and
lets reports answer them grows the reports. The questions are
specific: "What would success look like in 90 days?" "What's
the thing about this project that worries you?" "What do you
want to learn from this?"

**Giving honest feedback timely.** Withheld feedback compounds.
The report doesn't know they need to change; the gap widens;
the eventual conversation is harder than it should have been.
The discipline is feedback at the next 1-1, not feedback at
performance review.

**Setting clear expectations.** Ambiguity about what's
expected is the source of most performance issues. Concrete
expectations — what success looks like, what failure looks
like, how the report will be evaluated — eliminate a class of
problems.

**Making decisions.** Some decisions a manager must make are
unpopular. Avoiding them doesn't make them go away; it just
delays them. The discipline is making the decision when it
needs to be made.

## The 1-1 as the foundational ritual

Of all the rituals a manager runs, 1-1s are the most important.
A good 1-1 is the report's time, not the manager's. The
report brings the agenda. The manager listens, asks questions,
and gives feedback when relevant.

The patterns of good 1-1s are well known:

- Weekly or bi-weekly, 30 minutes.
- Cancellation is rare; rescheduling is preferred.
- Manager takes notes; report doesn't need to.
- Career conversations periodically; not every time.
- Feedback when relevant; not stockpiled for perf review.
- "Tell me more" is the manager's most common contribution.

Managers who do 1-1s poorly — or who skip them when busy —
are signaling that the report isn't a priority. The signal is
received.

## The hardest part: performance management

Most managers' fear is the performance management conversation.
Good managers have those conversations earlier and more
frequently than mediocre ones.

The pattern:

1. Notice the gap. Manager observes that something isn't
   meeting the bar.
2. Name the gap clearly to the report. Specific examples; not
   generalities.
3. Set concrete expectations for improvement, with timelines.
4. Check in regularly. Bi-weekly at minimum.
5. Document the conversation privately.
6. If improvement happens, acknowledge it.
7. If improvement doesn't happen, escalate to HR / PIP per
   company process.

Most performance problems caught and addressed early resolve.
Most performance problems avoided become eventual departures.

## The career conversation

Separately from performance, every report deserves regular
conversations about career growth. These are different
conversations: performance asks "are you meeting the current
bar?"; career asks "where do you want to go and how do we get
you there?"

Career conversations explore honestly, including paths that
lead the report away from your team. A manager who only ever
pitches paths within their own team comes across as self-
interested. The report notices, and trust degrades.

## What the lecture doesn't cover

This is the orientation. Specific topics — how to run a
performance improvement plan, how to handle reports going
through personal difficulties, how to fire someone humanely,
how to read engagement signals — each deserve their own
treatment.

## Further reading

- "High Output Management" — Grove. Foundational.
- "The Manager's Path" — Fournier. Engineering-specific.
- "Resilient Management" — Lara Hogan. Tactical and warm.
- "Radical Candor" — Kim Scott. Feedback frameworks.
- "Crucial Conversations" — Patterson et al. Hard conversations.
