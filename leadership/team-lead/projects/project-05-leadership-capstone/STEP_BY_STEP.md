# Step-by-Step — Project 05: Leadership Capstone

A 3-week guide for 40 hours of learner effort. Read it once before starting. Re-read the relevant week's section before you begin it.

Each week has: phase goals, time budget, daily-ish breakdown, deliverables produced, validation gates, common pitfalls.

---

## Pre-Work (before Week 1, ~3 hours)

Do this before the timer starts.

1. **Re-read your own deliverables from Projects 01-04.** (90 min.) Note down patterns in your decisions. Capture surprises, repeated themes, recurring framings. This is the source material for the Through-line.
2. **Skim two sources** (60 min):
   - Will Larson, *Staff Engineer*, ch. 6-8 (visibility, writing, brand).
   - Camille Fournier, *The Manager's Path*, ch. 6-8 (leading multiple teams, leading leaders).
3. **Pick the change-management subject** you'll use for the playbook's worked example. Options in `playbook.md` §1. Decide now; do not change mid-project.
4. **Schedule a 30-min slot with a peer team lead** (or simulate). They'll read your Leadership Design Doc at end of week 1 and give honest feedback.

If you skip pre-work you will spend Week 1 staring at a blank page instead of writing.

## Week 1 — Leadership Design Doc + Through-line (15 hours)

### Phase goal

By end of week, you have articulated your theory of leadership in writing. You have a 1-page Through-line connecting Projects 01-04. A peer team lead has read both and given feedback.

### Time budget

| Activity | Hours |
|---|---|
| Re-read own deliverables + pattern extraction | 2 |
| Through-line drafting | 2 |
| Leadership Design Doc — draft v1 | 5 |
| Peer review (give them the doc; have a conversation) | 1 |
| Leadership Design Doc — revision v2 | 2 |
| Through-line revision based on doc work | 1 |
| Buffer | 2 |
| **Total** | **15** |

### Daily-ish breakdown

**Day 1 — Pattern extraction.**

- Re-open Projects 01-04 deliverables side by side.
- For each project, ask: *what's the recurring move I made that I might not have noticed?*
- Capture 5-10 patterns. Look for the through-line — the one that shows up in all four.

**Day 2 — Through-line draft.**

- Use the template in `playbook.md` §3.
- The Through-line is a thesis, not a summary. State it as a claim.
- Identify what surprised you about the pattern.
- The surprise is often the highest-signal part.

**Days 3-4 — Leadership Design Doc draft v1.**

- Use the template in `playbook.md` §2.
- "What I optimize for" — ≤ 3 things. If you list more, you optimize for nothing.
- "What I've learned" — 4 specific moments, one per project. Before / after.
- "What kind of team I build" — observable behaviors only.
- "What I would do differently" — at least 2 specific decisions from past projects.
- "What's next" — the leadership skill you don't yet have. Real, not humblebrag.

**Day 5 — Peer review.**

- Hand the doc to a peer team lead (or simulate by reading it aloud and self-critiquing in the peer's role).
- Ask specifically: "Can you articulate my philosophy back to me in 1-2 sentences? Where does it sound generic? What's missing?"
- Capture feedback. Don't defend.

**Days 6-7 — Revision.**

- Cut every generic sentence.
- Replace abstract language with anchored examples.
- Re-read the Through-line — does the Design Doc match the thesis? Revise either or both.
- Final read: does it sound like you, or could it have been written by anyone? If anyone, rewrite.

### Deliverables produced

- **D1 — Leadership Design Doc** (final v2)
- **Through-line Statement** (final)

### Validation gate

**You cannot move to Week 2 until:**

- [ ] Leadership Design Doc has all required sections (optimize-for, learned, team-built, would-do-differently, what's-next).
- [ ] "What I optimize for" has ≤ 3 priorities, each observable.
- [ ] "What I've learned" references ≥ 2 specific moments from Projects 01-04.
- [ ] "What I would do differently" includes ≥ 2 specific past decisions.
- [ ] "What's next" passes the "uncomfortable to publish" test.
- [ ] Through-line statement is a thesis, not a recap.
- [ ] A peer (or your simulation) can articulate your philosophy in 1-2 sentences from the doc.

### Common pitfalls

- **Generic leadership writing.** "I value transparency and trust." Means nothing. Rewrite.
- **The accomplishments parade.** Listing what you delivered. Wrong project — that was the prior four.
- **Humblebrag growth edge.** "I sometimes care too much about quality." Reviewer caught this. Real growth edge.
- **Through-line as summary.** "I did A in P01, B in P02..." Not a through-line. A through-line is the *pattern across them*.
- **No anchored examples.** Abstract philosophy. Specifics or it didn't happen.

## Week 2 — Change-Management Playbook + Executive Briefing (15 hours)

### Phase goal

A peer leader could take your Change-Management Playbook and run a real change initiative with it. The incoming VP, reading your Executive Briefing as a pre-read, walks into your 60-minute meeting with 70% of the right picture.

### Time budget

| Activity | Hours |
|---|---|
| Change-management framework synthesis (Kotter + ADKAR + 7S + Switch) | 1 |
| Change-Management Playbook draft (all 7 sections) | 5 |
| Apply playbook to chosen specific change | 2 |
| Stakeholder coalition map for the specific change | 1 |
| Executive Briefing — pre-read | 1 |
| Executive Briefing — 60-min conversation arc | 2 |
| Anticipated VP questions + pre-baked answers | 1 |
| Speaker notes per section | 1 |
| Buffer | 1 |
| **Total** | **15** |

### Daily-ish breakdown

**Day 1 — Change-management framework synthesis.**

- Use `playbook.md` §4 as primer.
- Decide your hybrid: Kotter for macro arc + ADKAR for individual mapping + 7S for diagnosis + Switch for troubleshooting (or your own combo).
- Document which framework does what work in your playbook.

**Days 2-3 — Change-Management Playbook draft.**

- Use the template in `playbook.md` §5.
- Seven sections: propose, coalition, communicate, sequence, resistance, measure, cancel.
- Each section has templates / scripts, not just guidance.
- A peer must be able to use the playbook unchanged.

**Day 4 — Apply to specific change.**

- Pick the change you chose in pre-work (`playbook.md` §1 has options).
- Walk through every section of the playbook *for that specific change*.
- Produce a concrete rollout plan, not abstract guidance.
- Use the coalition mapping template in `playbook.md` §6.

**Day 5 — Executive Briefing — pre-read + arc.**

- 1-page pre-read covering team, top priority, top risk, top ask.
- 60-min conversation arc: first 15 (you + team), middle 30 (work + tensions), last 15 (asks + open questions).
- Use template in `playbook.md` §8.

**Day 6 — Anticipated questions + speaker notes.**

- ≥ 5 anticipated VP questions with pre-baked answers.
- Include the uncomfortable ones (use the bank in `playbook.md` §9).
- Speaker notes per section — what you'd actually say.

**Day 7 — Buffer + review.**

- Read both artifacts as if you were the audience.
- Cut anything that's filler.
- Check consistency: same leader voice in playbook and briefing.

### Deliverables produced

- **D2 — Change-Management Playbook** (final)
- **D3 — Executive Briefing** (final)

### Validation gate

- [ ] Change-management playbook covers all 7 sections with templates/scripts.
- [ ] Playbook applied to specific change produces concrete rollout plan.
- [ ] Coalition map identifies champions, opposers, undecided, unaware.
- [ ] Playbook references ≥ 1 established framework (Kotter / ADKAR / 7S / Switch / etc.).
- [ ] Cancellation criteria pre-committed.
- [ ] Executive briefing has 60-min arc with speaker notes.
- [ ] Pre-read is 1 page and would orient the VP before the meeting.
- [ ] ≥ 5 anticipated VP questions with pre-baked answers, including ≥ 1 uncomfortable.
- [ ] Briefing answers what team does, why matters, what's working, what's hard, what's needed from VP.

### Common pitfalls

- **Change playbook as framework recap.** "Kotter's 8 steps are..." Apply, don't enumerate.
- **No specific application.** Generic playbook reads as theoretical. Apply to a real (or worked-example) change.
- **Briefing as complaints session.** Don't complain about outgoing VP. Don't dump grievances. Frame as forward.
- **Briefing as accomplishments parade.** Wrong audience moment. Frame as priorities + tensions + asks.
- **Anticipated questions all softball.** Means you ducked the real ones.
- **Speaker notes as bullet points.** Speaker notes should be conversational — what you'd say, not what you'd present.

## Week 3 — Video Presentation + Portfolio Reflection (10 hours)

### Phase goal

You have a 15-minute video presentation outline (and ideally a recording) that lands a coherent leadership message. You have a portfolio index + reflection that names your real growth edge.

### Time budget

| Activity | Hours |
|---|---|
| Video presentation — thesis + arc | 2 |
| Video presentation — per-minute outline | 2 |
| Visual aids list + speaker prep | 1 |
| Anticipated audience questions | 1 |
| Portfolio index | 1 |
| Reflection — what changed, what wrong, growth edge | 2 |
| Final consistency pass across all 5 deliverables | 1 |
| Buffer | 0 (tight) |
| **Total** | **10** |

### Daily-ish breakdown

**Day 1 — Video presentation — thesis + arc.**

- Use the template in `playbook.md` §10.
- Thesis sentence: must land within first 2 minutes. Write it. Test it with a peer.
- Arc by minute (m0-m15). Use 3 stories (or 2 stories + 1 framework). Each story anchored, specific.
- Anticipated objection + your response at m13-m14.

**Day 2 — Per-minute outline + visual aids.**

- Flesh out beats and pacing per minute.
- Visual aids list: title + slide purposes. Slides ≠ message.
- Pre-warm-up routine for yourself.

**Day 3 — Audience Q&A + (optional) recording.**

- ≥ 5 anticipated audience questions with pre-baked answers.
- If recording: do it. The first take is rough; the third take is the keeper.
- Use the opening + closing patterns in `playbook.md` §11.
- Avoid the anti-patterns in `playbook.md` §12.

**Day 4 — Portfolio index.**

- Use the template in `playbook.md` §14.
- Each project: output (1 sentence), headline learning (1 sentence), link to files.
- Keep tight — this is an index, not a recap.

**Days 5-6 — Reflection + final pass.**

- Use the reflection prompts in `playbook.md` §13. Write privately first.
- Reflection section: what changed, what got wrong, what no longer believe, next growth edge.
- "Next growth edge" — passes the uncomfortable-to-publish test.
- Final pass: read all 5 deliverables end-to-end as one continuous portfolio. Cut redundancy. Check voice consistency (per `playbook.md` §15).

### Deliverables produced

- **D4 — Video Presentation Outline** (final; recording optional)
- **D5 — Portfolio Index + Reflection** (final)

### Validation gate

- [ ] Video outline has thesis in first 2 minutes.
- [ ] 3-5 supporting points, each anchored.
- [ ] Per-minute breakdown.
- [ ] ≥ 5 anticipated audience questions with answers.
- [ ] Visual aids are supplements, not message-carriers.
- [ ] Portfolio index lists all deliverables across all 5 projects.
- [ ] Reflection names ≥ 2 specific past decisions you'd reverse.
- [ ] Next growth edge is uncomfortable to publish.
- [ ] All 5 deliverables tell the same story. The leadership philosophy in D1 shows up in D3 and D4.

### Common pitfalls

- **Presentation as accomplishments parade.** Wrong genre.
- **Thesis hidden until minute 8.** Audience checked out. Open with the thesis.
- **Slides do the talking.** Voice-only listener should follow.
- **Audience questions all softball.** Means you ducked the real pushback.
- **Reflection as humblebrag.** "I sometimes care too much about quality" caught.
- **Voice changes across artifacts.** D1 sounds like one person, D4 like another. Means you don't have a settled voice yet — find it.

## What "Done" Looks Like

A peer team lead reading your Leadership Design Doc can describe your philosophy in 1-2 sentences from memory. An incoming VP reading your Executive Briefing as pre-read walks in with 70% of the right picture. A 15-minute video version of you describes who you are as a leader without listing accomplishments — the audience leaves with a sense of what you'd do in a hard moment. Your reflection names a growth edge you cannot yet fully address.

If you finish in less than 30 hours, you didn't push hard enough on the reflection or the executive briefing. Go back.

If you finish in more than 50 hours, you over-built the change-management playbook. Cut 20%.

If your capstone reads like five separate artifacts rather than one coherent leadership identity, you have not done the integration work. The capstone is the integration. That's the project.
