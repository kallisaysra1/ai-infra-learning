# Rubric — Project 05: Leadership Capstone

Six dimensions, each scored 1-5. Passing bar: average ≥ 4.0, no dimension < 3.

Each dimension lists sample evidence at each level. Use these to calibrate.

---

## Dimension 1 — Theory Clarity

*Does the Leadership Design Doc articulate a coherent theory of leadership — not a list of values, not a recap of accomplishments?*

| Level | Description | Sample evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Doc is a values list or a CV. | "I value transparency, trust, and excellence." No anchored examples. No specific behaviors. |
| 2 | Doc has structure but is generic. | Lists what they optimize for; could have been written by any team lead; no specifics from Projects 01-04. |
| 3 | Doc covers required sections (optimize-for, learned, team-built, would-do-differently, what's-next) with specifics. | "What I optimize for" has ≤ 3 things, each observable. "What I've learned" references ≥ 2 specific moments from prior projects. |
| 4 | L3 *plus* the doc has a clear voice. "What I would do differently" names ≥ 2 specific past decisions and why. "What's next" passes the uncomfortable-to-publish test. | "I escalate too late because I don't want to be perceived as not handling it." Specific decision named ("I should not have absorbed the on-call rebalance into a single sprint; should have separated rollout from buy-in"). |
| 5 | L4 *plus* the doc contains a leadership claim that the reader did not expect — a position that would generate productive disagreement among peer leaders. | A peer team lead can read the doc and say, "I don't lead this way, and here's why I think your approach has a specific cost — interesting." The doc has a position, not a consensus. |

## Dimension 2 — Through-line Coherence

*Do the previous 4 projects connect into one story? Is the through-line a thesis, not a summary?*

| Level | Description | Sample evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | No through-line, or "here's what I did in each project." | Reads as project recap. No pattern named. |
| 2 | Through-line stated but generic. | "I focused on people and process across all four projects." Could apply to any leader. |
| 3 | Through-line is a specific thesis with each prior project mapped to it. | "My recurring decision was to make implicit things explicit — non-goals, non-commits, anti-fit signals, rollback criteria." Mapped to all four projects. |
| 4 | L3 *plus* the doc names something that surprised the author about the through-line. | "I'd thought my superpower was clear thinking under ambiguity. The through-line says my superpower is actually forcing others to be explicit — and that has a cost." |
| 5 | L4 *plus* the through-line implies a specific growth edge that the rest of the portfolio reflects. | The through-line, growth edge, and next steps form a coherent forward arc; the leader has actually identified a next-level question, not just a current-level pattern. |

---

## Dimension 3 — Change-Management Practicality

*Would a peer leader use your Change-Management Playbook unchanged? Does the worked example produce a concrete rollout plan?*

| Level | Description | Sample evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Playbook is a framework recap (e.g., "Kotter's 8 steps are..."). | No application. No templates. No worked example. |
| 2 | Playbook has structure but is theoretical. | All 7 sections exist. No templates / scripts. No applied example. |
| 3 | Playbook covers all 7 sections with templates and scripts. Applied to a specific change. | Per `playbook.md` §5. Worked example for one of the candidate changes (or your own). |
| 4 | L3 *plus* coalition map for the specific change with named stakeholder categories. Cancellation criteria pre-committed. References established framework (Kotter / ADKAR / 7S / Switch). | "Strong opposers" identified by name (role-name); "what I'd propose" specific to each. |
| 5 | L4 *plus* the playbook handles the *interesting* failure modes of change initiatives — cancellation done well, resistance reframed from rational to environmental, the change announcement that names what stays the same as well as what changes. | "When to cancel" section is honest about the cost of well-executed cancellation; the change-announcement template includes explicit "what's not changing" — both rare and high-signal. A peer leader could run a non-trivial change end-to-end with this. |

## Dimension 4 — Executive Briefing Effectiveness

*Would the incoming VP leave the 60-minute meeting with the right understanding of your team, your priorities, and your asks?*

| Level | Description | Sample evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Briefing is unstructured, or a status update. | No conversation arc. No asks. No anticipated questions. |
| 2 | Briefing has structure but is one-directional. | "Here's what my team does. Here's what we're doing." No tensions named. No asks of the VP. |
| 3 | Briefing has 60-min conversation arc (first 15 / middle 30 / last 15) with speaker notes. Answers what team does, why matters, what's working, what's hard, what's needed from VP. | Per `playbook.md` §8. |
| 4 | L3 *plus* 1-page pre-read. ≥ 5 anticipated VP questions with pre-baked answers, including ≥ 1 uncomfortable. "Success at 6 months between us" framing. | "What's the question you're hoping I won't ask?" has a real answer. The briefing names tensions explicitly, not just achievements. |
| 5 | L4 *plus* "what I would NOT tell the VP in the first meeting" annex with reasoning. Voice is calibrated peer-to-peer, not deferential. The 1-page pre-read alone gives 70% of the right picture. | The briefing is something the VP would re-read after the meeting and find new information; the speaker notes feel like a confident senior peer, not a candidate auditioning. |

## Dimension 5 — Presentation Craft

*Is the 15-min talk well-structured, well-paced, and well-aimed at the audience? Does it land a coherent message?*

| Level | Description | Sample evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Outline is bullet-points, no narrative arc, no thesis. | Reads as a brain dump. Or: extreme accomplishments parade. |
| 2 | Outline has sections but pacing is unclear. | Thesis stated late or buried. No anticipated questions. |
| 3 | Outline has per-minute arc with thesis in first 2 minutes, 3-5 supporting points, close + CTA. ≥ 5 anticipated audience questions with answers. | Per `playbook.md` §10. |
| 4 | L3 *plus* anticipated objection + response at m13-m14. Visual aids supplement (not carry) the message. Audio-only listener could follow. Stories specific and anchored. | Recording (if optional) shows pacing within 30 sec of plan. Slides are minimal — title cards or single images. |
| 5 | L4 *plus* the talk has a position, not just an observation. Closes with a single-sentence takeaway the audience could repeat. The leader's voice is recognizable and confident. | If a peer leader saw the talk, they could say back the thesis in one sentence; the audience leaves with a sense of *what the leader would do in a hard moment*, not what they've done. |

---

## Dimension 6 — Reflection Honesty

*Does the reflection name a real growth edge? Does it pass the uncomfortable-to-publish test?*

| Level | Description | Sample evidence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reflection is humblebrag or absent. | "I sometimes care too much about quality." "I learned a lot." |
| 2 | Reflection is generic. | "I'd give myself more time to think strategically." Not specific. Not uncomfortable. |
| 3 | Reflection names ≥ 2 specific past decisions to reverse with reasoning. Growth edge is a specific skill. | "I would not have introduced the cadence and the on-call rebalance in the same week — they competed for the same trust budget." |
| 4 | L3 *plus* growth edge passes the uncomfortable-to-publish test. References a specific leadership skill the author cannot yet do well, with concrete development plan. | "I avoid the conversation where I tell a senior engineer that their behavior is hurting the team. I will practice this by [specific plan] over the next 6 months." |
| 5 | L4 *plus* a "what I no longer believe" section naming a leadership belief the author has explicitly moved past, *and* the reflection demonstrates the author can describe their own pattern from the outside — a meta-honesty rare in leadership writing. | "I used to believe that excellent process compensated for missing trust. I now believe trust precedes process. The on-call rebalance from Project 01 failed this test, and the change-management playbook in this capstone is in part me trying to internalize the lesson." |

## Reviewer Guidance

When reviewing, read in this order:

1. **Through-line statement first.** If it's a recap, the entire portfolio caps at ~3.
2. **Leadership Design Doc second.** Tells you whether the learner has a theory.
3. **Reflection section in D5 third.** Tells you whether the learner can see themselves clearly.
4. **Executive briefing fourth.** Tells you whether the learner can communicate to a senior audience.
5. **Change-management playbook fifth.** Tells you whether the learner can operationalize.
6. **Presentation outline last.** Tells you whether the learner can land a message.

Common reviewer mistakes:

- Grading on writing polish alone. A well-polished but generic doc is a 2; a slightly rough but honest doc is a 4.
- Missing the "uncomfortable to publish" test in the reflection. The single highest-signal element.
- Treating the playbook as a how-to manual to grade for completeness. Grade for applicability — would a peer use it on a real change?
- Grading the presentation on accomplishments rather than message. The presentation is about who the leader *is*, not what they've done.
- Forgetting that this is a portfolio, not 5 independent artifacts. Look for consistency across documents.
- Reading the artifacts at the wrong register. The Design Doc is a personal essay; the Executive Briefing is a peer memo; the Presentation Outline is spoken word. They sound different on purpose.

Anchor at L3 ("coherent and applied") and ask: what would push to L4 or L5? Provide written feedback per dimension, citing specific evidence. If you can't cite specific evidence for your score, the score is wrong.

The L5 evidence is almost always a *position*, a *surprise*, or an *honesty* the reviewer didn't expect to see. Look for those.
