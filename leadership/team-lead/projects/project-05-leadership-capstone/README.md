# Project 05 — Leadership Capstone

> **Track:** AI Infra Team Lead / Engineering Manager
> **Duration:** 40 hours (3 weeks, ~13 hrs/wk)
> **Type:** Portfolio & Presentation
> **Prereqs:** Projects 01-04 completed; all 4 modules (701-704) complete
> **Output:** A portfolio that demonstrates leadership integration — a design doc, change-management playbook, executive briefing, and a recorded video presentation outline

## 1. Project Summary

You've built the operating system (P01), written the strategy (P02), designed the hiring pipeline (P03), and led a cross-team initiative (P04). The capstone asks you to do something different: stop producing artifacts, start *presenting yourself as a leader*.

Your VP is moving on. The new VP arrives in 6 weeks and will be doing 60-minute introductions with each team lead. You have 60 minutes to position yourself, your team, and your work — and to set the trajectory of how the new VP will perceive you for the next 12 months.

Concurrently, you've been asked by your director to draft and present a change-management proposal for a non-trivial change to how engineering operates at the company. The director will use your draft as the basis for a leadership-team decision. Your draft has to survive that room.

Your job in this project: produce the artifacts that demonstrate you can think and communicate as a leader at the next level — not just execute as a strong team lead. Specifically:

1. **A leadership design doc** — a single artifact that articulates your *theory of leadership*: what you optimize for as a leader, what you've learned in this track, what kind of team and culture you build, and what you would do differently next time.
2. **A change-management playbook** — practical templates for proposing, communicating, and rolling out non-trivial organizational change.
3. **An executive briefing** — the 60-minute conversation with your incoming VP, structured as a written briefing plus speaker notes.
4. **A video presentation outline** — a 15-minute "this is who I am as a leader" presentation, suitable for the internal leadership development program or external promo-packet evidence.

This is the project that turns your previous four projects into a leadership identity.

## 2. Business Context

The transition from "doing the work" to "presenting the work" is the most-missed lesson in engineering leadership development.

For ML infrastructure leaders specifically:

- **Visibility lag.** ML infra work is invisible to executives until it breaks. If you cannot articulate it in non-technical language, your work is permanently underweighted in promotion conversations.
- **Strategic narrative.** A leader who can compress 12 months of work into 5 minutes is worth 3x a leader who needs 60 minutes for the same story.
- **Cross-org influence.** Senior leadership decisions are shaped by 1:1s, 15-minute hallway conversations, and pre-meetings. The leader who can move those moments is the one who shapes the org.
- **Personal brand inside the company.** Promotions don't happen because of work; they happen because of how a critical mass of senior people perceive the work. The capstone is about owning that.
- **Change resistance.** Most engineering organizations resist process change. A leader who can introduce change in a way that the org accepts is rare and valuable.

The artifacts in this project are not "extra" leadership theater. They are the difference between a team lead who delivers and a leader who shapes.

## 3. Learning Outcomes

By the end of this project you will be able to:

1. **Articulate** your theory of leadership in writing — what you optimize for, what you've learned, what kind of team you build.
2. **Synthesize** the through-line across your previous four projects into a single coherent leadership narrative.
3. **Design** a change-management initiative end-to-end, including communication strategy, stakeholder sequencing, and resistance handling.
4. **Brief** an executive (incoming VP) effectively in a 60-minute first conversation, with a structured artifact they can re-read.
5. **Present** yourself as a leader in a 15-minute talk — voice, narrative arc, anticipated questions.
6. **Reflect** on the limits of your current leadership style and articulate the next growth edge.

## 6. Week-by-Week Breakdown

### Week 1 — Leadership Design Doc + Through-line (15 hrs)

- **Goal:** Articulate your theory of leadership in writing. Connect Projects 01-04 into one narrative.
- Activities:
  - Re-read your own deliverables from Projects 01-04. Note recurring patterns in your decisions.
  - Write the Through-line Statement (1 page) connecting the four projects.
  - Write the Leadership Design Doc (4-6 pages). Sections: what I optimize for, what I've learned, what kind of team I build, what I would do differently, what's next.
  - Get feedback from one peer team lead and one skip-level (real or in simulation).
- Validation gate: A peer reading the design doc can articulate your leadership philosophy in 1-2 sentences. The Through-line statement is not a summary — it's a thesis.

### Week 2 — Change-Management Playbook + Executive Briefing (15 hrs)

- **Goal:** Produce two artifacts a peer leader could use without modification.
- Activities:
  - Write the Change-Management Playbook covering: how to propose change, how to communicate it, how to sequence the rollout, how to handle resistance, how to measure success.
  - Apply the playbook to a *specific* hypothetical change (e.g., "introducing a company-wide architecture review process," "shifting on-call ownership from teams to a central SRE function," "consolidating two ML infra teams").
  - Write the Executive Briefing for the incoming VP: 3-5 pages structured as a 60-minute conversation with explicit "first 15 / middle 30 / last 15" sequencing. Includes speaker notes and anticipated objections.
- Validation gate: The change-management playbook produces a concrete, sequenced rollout plan when applied to the specific change. The executive briefing answers what your team does, why it matters, what's working, what's hard, and what you need from the VP in their first 90 days.

### Week 3 — Video Presentation + Portfolio Reflection (10 hrs)

- **Goal:** Present yourself as a leader. Reflect on the limits of your current approach.
- Activities:
  - Write the 15-min video presentation outline: title, narrative arc, key points per minute, anticipated questions, visual aids list.
  - Record the video (if optional; highly recommended).
  - Write the Portfolio Index + Reflection — what you produced, what you learned, what your growth edge is.
  - Final read-through and consistency pass across all 5 deliverables.
- Validation gate: A non-technical viewer of the 15-minute presentation can describe back what kind of leader you are. The reflection names a real growth edge you don't yet know how to address.

## 7. Rubric (summary — full in `rubric.md`)

Graded across six dimensions, each 1–5:

1. **Theory clarity** — does the design doc articulate a coherent theory of leadership?
2. **Through-line coherence** — do the previous 4 projects connect into one story?
3. **Change-management practicality** — would a peer leader use your playbook unchanged?
4. **Executive briefing effectiveness** — would the incoming VP leave the 60-min meeting with the right understanding?
5. **Presentation craft** — is the 15-min talk well-structured, well-paced, and well-aimed?
6. **Reflection honesty** — does the reflection name a real growth edge?

Passing bar: 4.0 average, no dimension below 3.

## 8. Success Criteria

You have succeeded when:

- A peer team lead reading your Leadership Design Doc can describe your philosophy of leadership in 1-2 sentences from memory.
- An incoming VP reading your Executive Briefing in advance of a meeting with you would walk in with a 70% correct picture of your team and your priorities.
- A 15-minute video version of you describes who you are as a leader without listing accomplishments — the audience leaves with a sense of *what you'd do in a hard moment*, not what you've done.
- Your reflection names a leadership growth edge you cannot yet fully address. (If you can't name it, you don't see it.)
- A peer leader could take your Change-Management Playbook and run a change initiative with it next month.

## 11. A Note on the Genre Shift

The previous four projects asked you to produce operating documents — things teams *use*. The capstone asks you to produce *presentation* artifacts — things people *read about you*.

This is a different genre. The voice is different. The compression is different. The audience attention budget is different — you have 60 seconds for the most important thing, not 60 pages.

If you write the capstone with the same voice you used in the on-call playbook, you will fail it. The capstone is in the genre of executive memos, leadership essays, and TED-style talks. Read in that genre this week.

## 12. A Note on Vulnerability

The reflection section of the Leadership Design Doc and the Portfolio Reflection are the hardest parts of this project. They ask you to name what you don't yet know how to do.

The single most common failure mode is to write reflection as humblebrag — naming a "growth edge" that's actually a strength you're underselling ("I sometimes care too much about quality"). Your reviewer is calibrated to catch this.

Real growth edges look like: "I escalate too late because I don't want to be perceived as not handling it." "I have not yet hired or fired anyone." "I default to consensus when I should make the call." "I avoid the conversation where I tell the senior engineer that their behavior is hurting the team."

Write the real version.

## 13. A Note on the Video

A 15-minute video is the optional but most-recommended deliverable. The reason: the act of recording forces the artifact to land. You cannot hide behind written hedges. You cannot use "etc." or "and so on." You must say what you mean in your own voice.

If you skip the video, write the outline as if you will record it next week. The act of pretending the camera exists is itself the lesson.
