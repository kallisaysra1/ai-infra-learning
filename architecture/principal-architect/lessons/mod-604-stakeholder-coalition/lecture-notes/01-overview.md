# Lecture 01: Stakeholder Coalition Building — Overview

## Why this is a real skill

"Stakeholder coalition" sounds academic. The real subject
is *organizational power dynamics applied to architecture
decisions*. Principal architects who don't read politics
are blocked at every senior architecture review and don't
know why their proposals "go nowhere."

The truths to internalize:

- Good ideas don't sell themselves. A technically superior
  proposal that hasn't been pre-sold to three key
  stakeholders loses to a mediocre proposal that has.
- The first decision is who's in the room. A meeting with
  the wrong audience can't produce the right decision.
- Vetoes are silent until they matter. Security,
  compliance, procurement, finance can each kill a project.
- Coalitions decay. A coalition built in Q1 starts to
  fragment by Q3.
- Disagreement is information. A stakeholder pushing back
  knows something.

This lecture is the orientation: how to build coalitions
that survive contact with organizational reality.

## The stakeholder map

The artifact that makes coalition work concrete is the
stakeholder map. For each significant decision, you
identify:

**Decision-makers.** Who actually approves this?

**Veto holders.** Who can block it (security, legal,
procurement, finance)?

**Supporters.** Who will champion it in their team?

**Skeptics.** Who is likely to push back, and on what
grounds?

**Neutrals.** Who is currently neutral but could be moved
to either side?

The map is a working document. Names, positions, what each
person needs from you. It's not for sharing — it's the
principal architect's private analysis of the political
terrain.

## Moving the map

Building a coalition means moving people on the map. The
moves that work:

**Conversations before commitments.** Talk to each
decision-maker, veto holder, and skeptic *before* you put
the proposal up for decision. Tell them what you're
thinking. Ask what would change their mind. Listen.

**Address objections in the proposal.** When the proposal
goes out, the skeptics' concerns are already addressed in
writing. They notice. Many of them shift to neutral or
supportive simply because they were heard.

**Champion development.** Find the supporters early and
invest in the relationships. They'll carry the proposal in
rooms you're not in.

**Visible wins for veto holders.** Security wants
something. Procurement wants something. Finance wants
something. The proposal that gives each of them a visible
win clears their gates faster.

## The decision meeting

The decision meeting is the culmination of the coalition
work, not the start. By the time you walk in:

- Every key stakeholder has seen the proposal.
- Major objections have been addressed in writing.
- The decision-maker knows what they're being asked.

The meeting itself should be brief — most of the work
happened in 1-1s. The meeting ratifies the decision rather
than discovering it.

Common failure: treating the meeting as the moment of
persuasion. If you're persuading in the meeting, you've
already lost. Persuade in 1-1s; ratify in the meeting.

## Disagreement as information

Skeptics are the highest-signal information source you
have. They're the people who see the problems you've
missed. The mistake is treating them as obstacles.

The skill is treating them as collaborators:

- Ask what they're worried about.
- Take notes.
- Update the proposal to address the concerns.
- Acknowledge their contribution explicitly.

A skeptic whose concern has been addressed often becomes
a supporter. A skeptic who has been ignored becomes a
veto-holder.

## When coalitions decay

Coalitions don't last forever. People rotate, priorities
shift, urgency fades. The signs:

- The weekly sync stops happening.
- Champions stop bringing the initiative up.
- New people in stakeholder roles don't know the context.
- Decisions adjacent to the initiative get made without
  consulting it.

The principal architect's job is noticing the decay early
and renewing the coalition before it dissolves. The
maintenance work is real and ongoing.

## The political reality

This is the part of architecture that doesn't fit on a
diagram. The principal architects who succeed are those who
treat politics as a real input to their work — not as a
distraction from engineering, not as something to be
avoided, but as a skill to be developed.

The principle: politics serves the engineering, not the
other way around. Politics that becomes its own end is the
failure mode.

## What this lecture doesn't cover

This is the orientation. Specific topics — running 1-1 pre-
sells, structuring proposal docs to address objections,
escalating in difficult coalition situations — each
deserve their own treatment.

## Further reading

- "Getting to Yes" — Fisher & Ury. The classic on
  principled negotiation.
- "Crucial Conversations" — Patterson et al. Hard
  conversations.
- "Influence" — Cialdini. The psychology of persuasion.
- "The Hard Thing About Hard Things" — Horowitz.
  Executive-level political reality.
