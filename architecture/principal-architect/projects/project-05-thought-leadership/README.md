# Project 05: Thought Leadership Portfolio

**Duration**: 60 hours (8–10 weeks part-time, 1.5 weeks full-time)
**Difficulty**: Principal / Distinguished
**Impact tier**: Industry Impact (Tier 4)
**Project ID**: `project-05-thought-leadership`
**Related modules**: `mod-610-thought-leadership`, `mod-611-technical-writing`, `mod-612-public-speaking`, `mod-613-oss-strategy`, `mod-614-advisory-engagements`

## 1. Overview

You are a **Principal / Distinguished Engineer** in AI infrastructure with seven to twelve years of senior platform experience and a body of work — at one or more well-known companies — that the industry would recognize as substantial if it were visible. **It is not visible.** Your peers know your work; the broader industry does not. You have been doing exceptional work behind walls, and your career growth from this point requires the work to be **public, attributable, and influential**.

The next 12 months are the window to convert internal credibility into external authority. Your CTO has explicitly told you this is part of your promotion path to Distinguished (or your transition to a CTO / VP role at a peer firm, or your launch as an independent advisor / founder). You have the **time investment** approved: this is a sanctioned use of your work hours.

Your task is to design and execute a **thought leadership portfolio** across 5 channels:

1. **Conference talk** — proposed and accepted at a top-tier venue (KubeCon, AI Engineer Summit, MLOps World, Data + AI Summit, QCon, RAILS, or peer); delivered within 12 months
2. **Technical paper / long-form essay** — published at industry depth (USENIX, Communications of the ACM, IEEE software, ACM Queue, Anthropic / OpenAI engineering blogs by guest invitation, or a hosted essay with 50k+ views)
3. **Open-source contribution plan** — a sustained, meaningful contribution to a CNCF / LF AI / OSS-AI project that the maintainers would name you in
4. **Advisory engagement strategy** — 2–4 paid advisory engagements (board observer, technical advisor) closed within 12 months, with explicit anti-trust / non-compete management
5. **Content calendar** — a sustainable cadence of writing (newsletter, blog, social) that compounds reputation without becoming a second job

This is the **hardest project in the curriculum** for many senior engineers, because it requires skills that are not technical: framing for non-technical audiences, narrative structure, accepting public critique, managing the optics of changing jobs / earning side income / picking which company to consult for. The technical content is the easy part. The packaging, the politics, the cadence, and the sustained execution are where senior engineers stall.

The portfolio is graded not on volume but on **depth of impact per channel** and **coherence across channels**. A single conference talk that ten leaders cite as influential is worth ten generic-conference talks. A single paper that becomes a reference in adjacent companies' engineering blogs is worth a year of blog posts.

## 2. Career context

### 2.1 Why now

| Driver | Detail |
|---|---|
| Promotion path | Distinguished-level / VP / CTO-level roles require external visibility; internal-only careers cap at Principal in most large orgs |
| Market signal | The AI infrastructure community is forming public reputations very quickly; a 12-month delay is a 24-month opportunity cost |
| Compensation | Public reputation translates to 30–80% comp uplift in next role / advisory income |
| Optionality | Public reputation enables founder / VC / advisor optionality that internal-only careers do not have |
| Influence | Internal influence at one company; public influence at every company that reads your work |
| Recruiting | Your team's hiring funnel improves when senior engineers are publicly known |
| Industry contribution | The AI infra community needs senior voices; an absence is a cost |

### 2.2 Stakeholder map (compressed)

- **Executive sponsor**: Your CTO (or VP Eng); has explicitly approved the time investment
- **Internal**: Your manager (career conversation alignment); your CISO / CCO / legal (review for IP disclosure, NDA, employer commitments); your peers / team (you remain a contributor, not just a public figure)
- **External**: Conference program committees (CFP review); editors at journals / blogs; OSS project maintainers; future advisory clients; current / future employers
- **Counter-stakeholders**: Internal peers who view public exposure as opportunistic; OSS maintainers wary of drive-by contributors; conference reviewers fatigued by vendor pitches dressed as technical content; advisory clients who may have IP / competitive concerns with your employer

### 2.3 What success looks like (your acceptance criteria)

- 1 accepted conference talk at a tier-1 venue with delivered talk + recorded session; ≥ 800 in-room or ≥ 8,000 video views in trailing 90 days
- 1 published paper / long-form essay (≥ 3,500 words) at a venue with ≥ 50,000 organic reads or ≥ 30 inbound citations / references over 6 months
- 1 OSS contribution with maintainer recognition (named maintainer, committer access, or named in a release note for a substantial PR or design proposal accepted)
- 2 paid advisory engagements signed (or 1 board observer + 1 advisor), with proper legal review
- 12 published pieces of writing (newsletter / blog / long-form social) with a defined cadence (every 2–4 weeks) and ≥ 5,000 subscribers OR ≥ 50,000 trailing-12-month reads
- Zero IP / NDA / non-compete incidents — every external publication cleared by your employer's legal
- Internal sponsor confirms the public work has not diminished your internal contribution

## 3. Learning outcomes

By the end of this project, you can:

1. **Frame a technical contribution for a non-team audience** — talk titles that get accepted by program committees, paper abstracts that survive editor review, blog posts that get shared not skimmed
2. **Pick the right venue for the right contribution** — a KubeCon talk and a USENIX paper are not interchangeable; selection logic matters
3. **Sustain a writing cadence without burning out** — content calendar that withstands quarter-end crunch, vacation, illness, and the slow weeks
4. **Contribute meaningfully to OSS at the maintainer level** — not as a drive-by; with the patience and protocol the project's maintainers expect
5. **Close paid advisory engagements with proper legal cover** — equity vs. cash; scope; time commitment; conflict-of-interest management; existing-employer alignment
6. **Manage the politics of becoming publicly known** — at your current employer, with your peers, with adjacent companies, with your own future self
7. **Build a personal brand without becoming a brand** — your work is what is known; you are not a personality
8. **Operate the meta-loop** — measuring which contributions create the most leverage and reinvesting time into what compounds

## 4. Key questions you must answer

These appear in the rubric. Treat them as a writing prompt:

1. **What is your thesis?** A senior thought leader has one or two arguments they are publicly known for. What is yours? It must be specific enough that a peer can recite it; defensible enough that you will repeat it across multiple channels for years.
2. **Which conference talk, and why that venue?** Pick one specific venue with a specific CFP cycle; defend the venue choice (audience, prestige-to-effort ratio, network density).
3. **Which paper or essay, in which venue?** Same discipline.
4. **Which OSS project, and what is your contribution arc?** Specific project; specific contribution; specific maintainer relationship plan.
5. **Which advisory engagements, with what conflict-of-interest management?** Specifics with legal-review structure.
6. **What is your content calendar cadence and topic queue for 12 months?** Specifics including the "buffer pieces" that protect cadence in bad weeks.
7. **How do you handle the employer relationship?** Approval workflows, IP boundaries, scheduling, internal-vs-external balance.
8. **What is the **leverage metric** per channel — how do you know each channel is creating value, and what triggers a channel reallocation?

## 9. Success criteria

You're done when **all** of the following are true:

- A peer reading your D2 thesis can recite it back accurately and predict which conferences you'd target with it
- Your conference talk is **accepted** at a tier-1 venue (not just submitted) OR your CFP got a substantive reviewer-feedback rejection that you have revised against
- Your paper has been **published** OR is in editor review at a named venue with a reasonable timeline
- Your first OSS contribution is **merged** with a maintainer's name on the review thread, and your contribution arc is documented for the next 3 contributions
- Your advisory pipeline has ≥ 1 signed engagement (or term sheet in active negotiation), with your employer's legal cleared
- Your content calendar has 12 months of topics queued, with ≥ 4 pieces drafted as buffer
- Your employer's sponsor confirms in writing that the public work has not degraded your internal contribution
- A leverage-metrics review identifies the channel that is creating the most value and the channel that is creating the least — you would reallocate accordingly in cycle 2
